The International Price of a Dream:

how an international student made New York her home

By Irene Spezzamonte

When Ghita Bensilmane came to the United States the first time in 2011 her heart was beating at the rhythm of the Star-Spangled Banner. She had spent her entire childhood in an American school in her homecountry and when the moment to apply to college came, it was natural for her to start her American dream.

She then decided to go beyond her own dream and create more opportunity for international students at CUNY Graduate School of Journalism, where she is now a student

Originally from Casablanca, Morocco, Belsilmane came to the United States seven years ago. Her first american home was Boston

Bensilmane, then 20, decided to attend Boston University where she earned her Bachelor's degree. But after earning diploma,she was forced to go back to her native country because her visa even though she had the possibility to have a job at Snapchat.

International students have to find a company that is willing to support a H-1b visa, also known as working visa, in order to be able to work and live in the United States.

Against Bensilmane's hopes, Snapchat was not able to sponsor her visa.

Thousands of international students apply every year for an H-1b visa, but only a specific number gets approved through a lottery system under the Immigration Act of 1990.

The year Bensilmane left the country, 2016, the United States Citizenship and Immigration Services received a total of 233,000 H-1B petitions, but only 65,000 were approved.

One year later, Bensilmane decided to come back to the United States to pursue her dream and so she enrolled for the Master’s degree in social journalism at CUNY Graduate School of Journalism.

This time she came to the big apple to stay.

She wasn’t thinking only about herself, however. She wanted to bring a change to the school that would have helped all international students at CUNY-J.

Ghita Bensilmane, 24, has been living the American dream since she entered the school system in Morocco, Photo Courtesy Ghita Bensilmane

Belsilmane decided to pen a letter to Dean Sarah Bartlett asking to designate the Master’s degree as S.T.E.M. This would have allowed international students to extend their work permit after graduation by two extra years.

International students can indeed apply for Optional Practical Training as soon as they graduate from an American college. The OPT is usually one year-long. However, if you graduate with a STEM degree you are allowed to extend your OPT for an additional two years making it a total of three years during which an international students can stay in States and work. This is a great advantage for the 1 million international students currently studying in the country because under an international student visa it is not possible to work.

The restrictive laws and the election of Donald Trump had an impact on international students enrollement in the United States. Data from the United States Department of State - Bureau of Consular Affairs shows that the number of international students enrolling in American universities declined by 25 percent. Having the opportunity to stay in the country a work for an extended period of time is an advantage for those international students who wish to build a career in the States.

When the news broke that CUNY-J became a STEM designated program, the response from international students was overwhelming.

“This is really life changing for all of us international students. At this moment, I am very emotional and can't quite articulate what this feels.” Maria Montanez wrote.

“This really changes our lives and careers, and our families will thanks you forever.” Costanza Gallardo added.

"It feels unfair that after four years of studying in the US they government kind of looks at you and says ‘thank you for your tuition, goodbye’ so that’s why I decided to do something about it."

But Bensilmane was not done. She brought that change to Texas after she met Olivia Rempel, a fellow international student at UC Berkeley, while she was getting trained as a student leader with ProPublica's Documenting Hate project. There she told her about the S.T.E.M. designation and provided her with some advice on how to change the designation program at Berkley.

“After months of hard work by Olivia and her colleagues, UC Berkeley's journalism department has announced its programs would be getting the STEM designation, meaning current and former students will now get three years after school.” Bensilmane said.

“Moral of the story: if you're an international student in the US, and you hope to gain substantial work experience here after college, you may want to look into whether or not your program may qualify as STEM.” Bensilmane said. “Laslty, if you hear any talk of Trump trying to take away the 2-year STEM extension, fight it with all your might! Shut it down!”
